Where demand rose, real prices held.
U.S. states: electricity demand change vs. real residential price change, 2019–2025, and bottom quintile energy burden.
EIA retail sales (deflated by CPI-U); U.S. Census ACS PUMS.

Methodology & data

Across states, demand growth and real residential price growth are mildly negatively correlated (Pearson r ≈ −0.45). The fastest-demand-growth states have largely held real prices flat or lower, while the largest real increases are in states where demand was flat or falling. The reasons for rising energy prices are multi-factorial. This analysis will be updated as more recent data is uploaded to EIA.

Sources: EIA, Electricity Sales, Revenue, and Average Price, via the EIA Open Data API (2019–2025); CPI-U, All Items (FRED, CPIAUCSL) for the inflation adjustment; U.S. Census Bureau, ACS PUMS (2024) for energy burden.

Texas built capacity faster than demand grew, and real prices held
Texas electricity demand, supply (installed capacity), and real residential price, indexed to 2015 = 100. Demand rose 28.8% while Texas added installed capacity faster still (+43.7%, ~51 GW); the real (inflation-adjusted) residential price fell 2.4%.
Source: EIA — total retail electricity sales (demand), installed net summer capacity (supply), and residential price deflated by CPI-U (real, constant 2024 dollars).

Methodology & data

All three series are indexed to 2015 = 100. Demand is total retail electricity sales (all sectors); supply is installed generating capacity (net summer capability, all fuels, all sectors); price is the average residential price in constant 2024 dollars (CPI-U, all items). We use installed capacity rather than net generation as the supply measure: net generation moves with demand almost by identity, so it can't independently show that supply kept pace, while capacity is the stock of productive capital. (Texas net generation, an output measure, grew about 26% over this window.) Over the decade Texas added roughly a third more demand and built capacity faster still, while real residential prices ended slightly lower. Sources: EIA Electricity Sales, Revenue, and Average Price and State Electricity Profiles (net summer capability), via the EIA Open Data API (2015–2024); CPI-U, All Items (FRED, CPIAUCSL).

Texas built new supply. The country mostly didn't.
Installed generating capacity, indexed to 2015 = 100. Over the decade Texas grew capacity 43.7% (~51 GW); the U.S. as a whole grew just 15.6% — even as forecasters now expect demand to surge.
Source: EIA installed net summer capacity (all fuels, all sectors). Load-growth forecast: Grid Strategies (2025).

Methodology & data

Both lines are installed generating capacity (net summer capability, all fuels, all sectors), indexed to 2015 = 100. Texas grew capacity 43.7% (~51 GW) over the decade — outpacing its ~29% demand growth — while U.S. capacity grew just 15.6%. We use installed capacity, not net generation, as the supply measure: generation tracks demand almost by identity and can't show whether supply kept pace. The U.S. added about 166 GW of capacity over this entire decade, yet Grid Strategies now projects roughly that same 166 GW of load growth in just five years — so meeting it will require building much faster, closer to Texas's pace than the country's recent average. Sources: EIA State Electricity Profiles (net summer capability), via the EIA Open Data API (2015–2024); Grid Strategies, Power Demand Forecasts Revised Up for Third Year Running (2025).
